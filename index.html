Three ways I embed sustainability into a business

01 · Operating model

Sustainability is not a side-of-desk function. Done right, it operates as a risk lens, a growth lens, and a brand lens concurrently inside the business.

Risk reduction vehicle

Stay ahead of regulatory change, avoid fines and disclosure failures, and hedge against material-price shocks driven by climate, energy, and resource scarcity.

Compliance is a floor, not a ceiling. The goal is to convert regulatory pressure into operational discipline before it converts into cost.

Innovation opportunity creator

Reframe constraints into commercial opportunity. Sustainability pressure can unlock new revenue streams, lower input costs, surface adjacent markets, and sharpen product differentiation.

For example, in food manufacturing, value recovery from waste streams (circular economy), packaging redesign, and lower-impact ingredient sourcing become commercial moats rather than cost lines.

Branding benefit

Build a credible social licence to operate, the unwritten permission from customers, regulators, communities, and capital markets that lets a business grow.

Done well, it opens green-bond and impact-fund capital, lifts customer lifetime value, and gives talent a reason to stay.

How sustainability adds value to the business

01a · The frame

Every business operates inside a constantly changing environment. Sustainability is about positioning the business to capture value from that change rather than be eroded by it. The work sits in the zone between the business and the world it depends on.

Risks left unmanaged become costs. Opportunities left uncaptured become competitor advantages. The job is to read the environment early, build internal capability to respond, and turn that into commercial outcomes.

My journey

06 · The why

My love for nature started early. Both of my parents are scientists, and I got properly hooked the day they handed me my first nature book. More followed, and I sat in awe of how the planet formed, how ecosystems hold together, and how today's species came to be. That curiosity built a real thirst for knowledge.

I lost some of that spark through my schooling years. I was lucky enough to intern across law, consulting, banking, and FMCG, and learned a lot in each. But what actually pulled me back in was the work I did pro bono for mission-driven organisations. That was where I rediscovered why I cared.

What I care about now is positive social and environmental change at the systems level. There's a long way to go, and the hurdles are genuinely complex. The more I learn about the economic, regulatory, financial, and psychological factors that drive how individuals and systems actually shift, the more I want to put that knowledge to work creating a better future for every living thing on this planet.
